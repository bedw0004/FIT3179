Net Zero: a renewable energy future

The consequences of climate change are becoming increasingly evident and severe.

The world's energy requirements are causing rising temperatures, extreme weather events, and the melting of polar ice caps. To avoid the worst outcomes, the world needs to act now, but we aren't.

So what does the data say about the current state of affairs in the energy sector?

And is there any evidence to support the current climate inaction?

The twin energy problem

To bring emissions down towards net-zero is arguably the world’s biggest challenge in the short-term future.

But the world’s energy problem is actually even larger than that, because the world has not one, but two energy problems.

1. Developing countries with low carbon emissions lack access to energy. This is seen in the left-hand side of the scatter plot.

2. Richer countries produce excessive greenhouse gas emissions. This is seen in the right-hand side of the scatter plot.

Consumption-based CO₂ emissions per capita vs. GDP per capita

The line charts below show the downward trend of CO2 emissions per capita in the countries with higher GDP per capita in recent years.

While in countries with lower GDP per capita, the emissions curve is flattening, but is not decreasing.

Richer countries need to help developing nations to transition their energy habits if the world is to have any hope of meeting its emissions targets.

The obligation is on the more fortunate of us, to help those who aren't so lucky.

The current energy breakdown

Energy consumption by source

Fossil fuels make up a majority of the energy consumption worldwide.

Oil is consumed massively by the transport industry in road vehicles and on the air and sea.

Electricity production is another huge consumer of energy. This is where coal has the trump card In recent years solar and wind have been steadily increasing in their share.

Renewable is better, full stop

Renewable sources in the energy mix

It is easy to see why solar and wind power plants have increased in the world's energy mix in the last few years.

New solar and wind energy plants are by far the cheapest to install, a no brainer for new investment.

And, renewable energy and nuclear power plants are by far the safest and cleanest source of energy.
