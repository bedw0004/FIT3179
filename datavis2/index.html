Net Zero: a renewable energy future

The consequences of climate change are becoming increasingly evident and severe.

The world's energy requirements are causing rising temperatures, extreme weather events, and the melting of polar ice caps. To avoid the worst outcomes, the world needs to act now, but we aren't.

So what does the data say about the current state of affairs in the energy sector?

And is there any evidence to support the current climate inaction?

The twin energy problem

To bring emissions down towards net-zero is arguably the world’s biggest challenge in the short-term future.

But the world’s energy problem is actually even larger than that, because the world has not one, but two energy problems.

1. Developing countries with low carbon emissions lack access to energy. This is seen in the left-hand side of the scatter plot.

2. Richer countries produce excessive greenhouse gas emissions. This is seen in the right-hand side of the scatter plot.

The current energy breakdown

Where do the world's CO₂ emissions come from?

The majority of the world's emissions come from industry. With byproducts from mining and chemical processes and gigantic servers running for digital companies, it's no surprise that industry makes up 29.4% of our emissions.

Buildings produce emissions through lighting and heating/cooling. Whilst with agriculture it's largely methane from livestock. The transport industry uses a majority of the world's oil to produce its emissions, with cars, trucks, planes and ships being the major contributors.

What sources form the world's electricity production?

Electricity production is a huge consumer of energy and cause of CO₂ emissions.

Coal is clearly the biggest player, with over 1/3 of the world's total electricity consistently coming from coal power plants in the last 30 years.

The rise of gas has come with advances in gas power plants that allow for the quick ramping up and down in pace with energy grid requirements.

On the renewable front, solar and wind have steadily increased their share in recent years. From virtually non-existent in the 90s, to over 12% of the world's share in 2022.

However, due to the increased vilification of nuclear, due to newsworthy risks such as meltdowns in Chernobyl and Fukushima, its share has significantly decreased from 17.0% to 9.1% over just 30 years.

How are individual countries forming their electricity percentages?

In recent years it is clear to see the downward trend in fossil fuel use. However, these toxic chemicals still make up a large percentange of the energy mix world wide.

In particular, Australia, India, South Africa and USA all produce around 2/3 of their electricity from fossil fuels.

Countries such as Norway and Finland should be the world's role models on the renewable energy front.

It is unfortunate to see again the reduction in nuclear energy production, particularly in France.

Renewable is better, full stop

Energy production from renewable sources

Price of electricity from new power plants ($/MWh)

The relative explosion in both wind and solar energy production worldwide can be explained by the dramatic reduction in Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) of the two sources. LCOE is a measure produced by Lazard, 2021 that estimates the cost of energy from new power plants built today.

Whilst fossil fuel plants such as coal and gas have remained stagnant in recent years, solar has dropped from $359/MWh in 2009, to just $36/MWh in 2021 and wind has dropped from $135/MWh in 2009 to just $38/MWh in 2021.

The safest and cleanest sources of energy

Not only are renewable sources now cheaper than fossil fuels, they are also safer!

In the butterfly chart below, deaths from air pollution and accidents in the supply chain are considered.

Coal and oil are orders of magnitude more dangerous for humans than other sources. With millions that die every year due to health impacts of air pollution.

Nuclear deaths are largely influenced by the two prominent accidents in recent history (Chernobyl and Fukushima). The same goes for hydropower with the single Banqiao Dam Failure in 1975 killing approximately 171,000 people.

Yet again, solar and wind energy prove to be the best sources of energy. With the lowest deaths rates and emissions.

Solar, wind and nuclear's prominence should be increased in every countries energy mix.

There are a number of different and effective techniques for reaching net zero:

Norway has a very high percentage of hydropower plants with its unique environment low to the sea. France has traditionally been very nuclear friendly. Greenland has ironically used climate change to its advantage with its glacial meltwater hydro plants.

Meanwhile, as a positive sign of what might be to come in the western world, the UK has greatly upped its production through wind and bioenergy, with a large reduction in its reliance on coal.
